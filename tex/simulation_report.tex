\section{Model Description}
\label{sec:model}

We simulate a two-type continuous-time branching process with cell types $X$
and $Y$.  Each cell divides independently and exponentially:

\begin{align}
  X \to X + X & \quad \text{rate } a \text{ per } X \text{ cell,} \\
  X \to X + Y & \quad \text{rate } b \text{ per } X \text{ cell,} \\
  Y \to Y + X & \quad \text{rate } c \text{ per } Y \text{ cell,} \\
  Y \to Y + Y & \quad \text{rate } d \text{ per } Y \text{ cell.}
\end{align}

Each division event creates exactly one new cell of the appropriate type;
the parent cell is not removed.  All four Poisson clocks are independent.
Events are sampled exactly using the Gillespie
algorithm~\cite{gillespie1977,gillespie1976}: at each step the time to the
next event is drawn from an exponential distribution with rate equal to the
total propensity, and the event type is chosen proportionally to the
individual propensities.

Throughout this analysis the parameters are fixed at
$a = 0.5$, $b = 0.1$, $c = 0.05$, $d = 0.4$, with initial condition
$n_X(0) = 1$, $n_Y(0) = 0$.  The process is stopped when the \emph{total}
cell count across all pools exceeds $N = 1000$.

\subsection{Theoretical long-run composition}

The mean dynamics satisfy
\[
  \frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix}X\\Y\end{pmatrix}
  = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}a & c \\ b & d\end{pmatrix}}_{A}
    \begin{pmatrix}X\\Y\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The dominant eigenvalue of $A$ determines the exponential growth rate and
the corresponding eigenvector determines the long-run composition:

\[
  \lambda_1
  = \frac{(a+d) + \sqrt{(a-d)^2 + 4bc}}{2}
  = \frac{0.9 + \sqrt{0.03}}{2}
  \approx 0.537.
\]

The stable-composition eigenvector satisfies
$(A - \lambda_1 I)\mathbf{v} = 0$, giving

\[
  \frac{Y}{X}
  = \frac{\lambda_1 - a}{c}
  = \frac{0.037}{0.05}
  \approx 0.732,
  \qquad
  \frac{X}{X+Y}
  \approx 0.578.
\]

These values are the theoretical equilibrium targets regardless of initial
conditions.


% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Theoretical Variance of the Two Ratios}
\label{sec:variance}

\subsubsection{Moment equations for a single pool}

Because every cell divides independently, the process is a \emph{multi-type
	linear birth process} and its moment equations can be derived from the master
equation.  Define, for a pool starting from a single type-$X$ founder:
\[
\mu_X(t) = E[X(t)], \quad \mu_Y(t) = E[Y(t)],
\]
\[
\sigma_{XX}(t) = \operatorname{Var}(X(t)), \quad
\sigma_{YY}(t) = \operatorname{Var}(Y(t)), \quad
\sigma_{XY}(t) = \operatorname{Cov}(X(t), Y(t)).
\]

The first moments satisfy
\begin{equation}
	\frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix}\mu_X\\\mu_Y\end{pmatrix}
	= A\begin{pmatrix}\mu_X\\\mu_Y\end{pmatrix},
	\qquad A = \begin{pmatrix}a & c \\ b & d\end{pmatrix},
	\label{eq:mean_ode}
\end{equation}
so $(\mu_X(t),\mu_Y(t))^{\top} = e^{At}(1,0)^{\top}$.

Applying the generator to $X^2$, $XY$, and $Y^2$ and subtracting the square
of the mean yields the \emph{second central moment ODE system}:
\begin{align}
	\frac{d\sigma_{XX}}{dt} &= 2a\,\sigma_{XX} + 2c\,\sigma_{XY}
	+ (a\mu_X + c\mu_Y), \label{eq:sxx}\\
	\frac{d\sigma_{XY}}{dt} &= b\,\sigma_{XX} + (a+d)\,\sigma_{XY}
	+ c\,\sigma_{YY}, \label{eq:sxy}\\
	\frac{d\sigma_{YY}}{dt} &= 2b\,\sigma_{XY} + 2d\,\sigma_{YY}
	+ (b\mu_X + d\mu_Y), \label{eq:syy}
\end{align}
with initial conditions $\sigma_{XX}(0)=\sigma_{XY}(0)=\sigma_{YY}(0)=0$.
This is a \emph{linear} system driven by $(\mu_X,\mu_Y)$, so it can be
integrated exactly (e.g.\ via \texttt{scipy.integrate.odeint}).

For a pool starting with $n_0$ type-$X$ founders, independence of individuals
gives
\[
\operatorname{Var}(X(t)) = n_0\,\sigma_{XX}(t), \quad
\operatorname{Var}(Y(t)) = n_0\,\sigma_{YY}(t), \quad
\operatorname{Cov}(X(t),Y(t)) = n_0\,\sigma_{XY}(t).
\]

\subsubsection{Delta method for the ratio variances}

Let $N = X + Y$, $\alpha = \mu_X/(\mu_X+\mu_Y)$, $\beta = \mu_Y/(\mu_X+\mu_Y)$,
and $r = \mu_Y/\mu_X$.  Applying the delta method to the two ratios:

\begin{equation}
	\operatorname{Var}\!\left(\frac{X}{X+Y}\right)
	\approx
	\frac{\beta^2\,\sigma_{XX} + \alpha^2\,\sigma_{YY}
		- 2\alpha\beta\,\sigma_{XY}}{(\mu_X+\mu_Y)^2},
	\label{eq:var_r1}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
	\operatorname{Var}\!\left(\frac{Y}{X}\right)
	\approx
	\frac{r^2\,\sigma_{XX} + \sigma_{YY} - 2r\,\sigma_{XY}}{\mu_X^2}.
	\label{eq:var_r2}
\end{equation}

Both approximations are first-order and accurate when the population is large
relative to its standard deviation.

\subsubsection{Long-run behavior: spectral gap and concentration}

The homogeneous part of the second-moment system \eqref{eq:sxx}--\eqref{eq:syy}
has eigenvalues $\{2\lambda_1,\; \lambda_1+\lambda_2,\; 2\lambda_2\}$.
Because $\lambda_1 > \lambda_2$, the solution grows as $e^{2\lambda_1 t}$ for
large $t$, so $\sigma_{XX}, \sigma_{YY}, \sigma_{XY} \sim C\,e^{2\lambda_1 t}$.
At the same time $\mu_X, \mu_Y \sim C' e^{\lambda_1 t}$, and hence
$(\mu_X+\mu_Y)^2 \sim e^{2\lambda_1 t}$.

The numerator and denominator of \eqref{eq:var_r1} thus both grow as
$e^{2\lambda_1 t}$, which would naively suggest a constant limiting variance.
However, the high positive correlation between $X$ and $Y$ (both driven by the
same Malthusian martingale~\cite{kesten1966,athreya1972}
$W = \lim_{t\to\infty} e^{-\lambda_1 t}
\mathbf{u}^{\top}\mathbf{Z}(t)$, where $\mathbf{u}$ is the left eigenvector
of $A$ normalised so that $\mathbf{u}^{\top}\mathbf{v}_1 = 1$) makes the numerator
$\beta^2\sigma_{XX}+\alpha^2\sigma_{YY}-2\alpha\beta\sigma_{XY}$ grow
\emph{slower} than $e^{2\lambda_1 t}$.

The correct large-$t$ result, derived from the spectral decomposition, is
\begin{equation}
	\operatorname{Var}\!\left(\frac{X}{X+Y}\right)
	\;\sim\; K\,e^{-2\varepsilon t},
	\qquad \varepsilon = \lambda_1 - \lambda_2
	= \sqrt{(a-d)^2+4bc}
	\approx 0.173,
	\label{eq:var_asymp}
\end{equation}
with an analogous decay for $\operatorname{Var}(Y/X)$.
The variance therefore decays \emph{exponentially} at a rate equal to twice
the \textbf{spectral gap} $\varepsilon$ between the two eigenvalues of $A$.

Intuitively, the fluctuation in $X/(X+Y)$ is driven by the sub-dominant
eigenvector component of $\mathbf{Z}(t)$, which decays relative to the
dominant term at rate $e^{(\lambda_2-\lambda_1)t} = e^{-\varepsilon t}$.
Because both $X$ and $Y$ share the same dominant Malthusian factor $W$,
the $W$-randomness cancels in the ratio, and only the sub-dominant residual
contributes to the variance.

\paragraph{Practical implication.}
Parameters with a \emph{larger} spectral gap (e.g.\ increasing $a-d$ or
decreasing the cross-production rates $b,c$) lead to faster ratio convergence
and smaller variance at any given time.  With our parameters,
$\varepsilon \approx 0.173$, so the ratio variance halves roughly every
$\ln 2/(2\varepsilon) \approx 2.0$ time units.

\subsection{Implications for the split simulation}

When multiple independent pools are combined, the variance of the
\emph{combined} ratio is reduced by an additional factor.  If there are $K_p$
pools each of approximate size $N/K_p$, and the pools are independent, then
by the law of total variance the combined ratio variance is reduced by
a factor of approximately $1/K_p$ relative to a single pool of the same total
size — provided the seeding probability $p$ does not introduce systematic bias.
When $p \neq \alpha_{\text{eq}}$ (the equilibrium fraction of $X$), successive
pool resets introduce a systematic shift in composition, and the effective
variance can actually \emph{increase} in the early transient phase before the
pools equilibrate.

\section{Single-Pool Baseline}
\label{sec:single}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig_single.pdf}
  \caption{Single-pool Gillespie simulation~\cite{gillespie1977} ($N = 1000$, $n_X(0)=1$,
           $n_Y(0)=0$).  Top: counts of $X$ and $Y$ over time.
           Bottom: ratios $X/(X+Y)$ and $Y/X$; dashed lines show
           theoretical equilibrium values.}
  \label{fig:single}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:single} shows the baseline single-pool simulation.
Key observations:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Exponential growth.}  Starting from a single $X$ cell the
        total population grows exponentially.  The growth is initially
        stochastic; once the population is large the law of large numbers
        removes visible fluctuations.

  \item \textbf{X initially dominates.}  Because the process starts as pure
        $X$ and $a \gg b$, $X$ cells accumulate rapidly before $Y$ cells
        appear via $X \to Y$ events.

  \item \textbf{Convergence of ratios.}  Both $X/(X+Y)$ and $Y/X$ converge
        toward the theoretical equilibrium ($\approx 0.578$ and
        $\approx 0.732$ respectively), illustrated by the dashed reference
        lines.  The convergence is not monotone because of stochastic
        fluctuations early in the process.

  \item \textbf{Long-run Y/X $< 1$.}  $Y$ cells never exceed $X$ cells in
        this regime.  This follows directly from $a > d$ (X self-renews
        faster than Y) and the relative smallness of the cross-production
        rates $b$ and $c$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Pool-Splitting Simulation}
\label{sec:split}

\subsection{Splitting mechanism}

When the total cell count in a pool reaches the threshold $K$, one cell is
removed and placed into a new, independent pool.  The removed cell is of
type $X$ with probability $p$ and type $Y$ with probability $1 - p$.
The parent pool continues with $K - 1$ cells.

In \emph{sequential} mode (used here) pools are processed via a first-in-first-out (FIFO) queue:
the active pool is simulated until it either reaches $K$ or goes extinct,
then both the parent (now $K-1$ cells) and the new child (1 cell) are placed
at the end of the queue.  The cumulative inter-event waiting times define a
global time axis shared across all pools.

Note that a split does not change the total cell count; only division events
do.  Therefore the global exponential growth rate $\lambda_1$ is unaffected
by $K$ and $p$; what changes is the \emph{composition} trajectory.

\textbf{Small $K$ (frequent splits).}  With $K = 10$, each pool only
undergoes $K - 1 = 9$ divisions before it splits.  Starting from a single
cell this is rarely enough time to approach the equilibrium composition.
Consequently:

\begin{itemize}
  \item More pools are created (lower $K$ $\Rightarrow$ more splits for the
        same $N$), each inheriting a near-pure founder cell.
  \item The founder effect is strong: a pool seeded by a single $X$ cell will
        be $\sim$85\% $X$ by the time it reaches $K = 10$ (since $a/(a+b)
        \approx 0.83$).
  \item The combined ratios fluctuate more and can deviate substantially from
        equilibrium, especially when $p \ne 0.5$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Large $K$ (infrequent splits).}  With $K = 100$, each pool runs long
enough for the composition to approach the eigenvector equilibrium before it
splits.  As a result:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Fewer pools are created, but each has a richer history.
  \item The cell sampled for transfer is drawn from a near-equilibrium pool,
        so it is $X$ with probability $\approx 0.578$ regardless of $p$
        (as long as $p$ is not extreme).
  \item The combined ratios converge more smoothly toward $0.578$ and
        $0.732$, resembling the single-pool behaviour.
\end{itemize}

The cross-$K$ ratio comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:K_comp}) confirms that
larger $K$ brings the trajectories closer to equilibrium earlier in the
process.

\subsection{Effect of seeding probability $p$}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{fig_p_comparison.pdf}
  \caption{Cross-$p$ comparison of ratios at $K = 50$.  Left:
           $X/(X+Y)$.  Right: $Y/X$.  Dashed grey line: theoretical
           equilibrium.}
  \label{fig:p_comp}
\end{figure}

The seeding probability $p$ determines which cell type founds each new pool
and therefore strongly influences the transient composition, especially for
small $K$.

\textbf{$p = 1$ (always seed with $X$).}  Every new pool starts as a pure
$X$ colony.  Since $a \gg b$, $X$ self-renews quickly and $Y$ cells
accumulate slowly.  The combined $X/(X+Y)$ is pushed above the equilibrium
value $0.578$, and $Y/X$ is correspondingly suppressed below $0.732$.

\textbf{$p = 0$ (always seed with $Y$).}  Every new pool starts as pure $Y$.
Since $d \gg c$, $Y$ self-renews strongly and $X$ cells grow slowly from
$Y \to X$ events.  The combined $X/(X+Y)$ is pushed \emph{below} $0.578$,
sometimes falling well below $0.5$ for small $K$.

\textbf{$p = 0.5$ (equal probability).}  Seeding is symmetric: roughly half
of new pools are $X$-seeded and half are $Y$-seeded.  The composition
averages out, and the combined ratios converge toward equilibrium faster
than the extreme cases.

Figure~\ref{fig:p_comp} shows this pattern clearly for $K = 50$:
$p = 1$ keeps $X/(X+Y)$ elevated; $p = 0$ depresses it; $p = 0.5$ lies
between the two and reaches the dashed equilibrium line soonest.

\section{Parallel Splitting}
\label{sec:parallel}

\subsection{Mechanism}

In \emph{parallel} mode all pools advance simultaneously on a shared,
real-valued time axis.  A global min-heap orders every pool's next potential
division event by its scheduled time; at each step the soonest event fires,
the affected pool is updated, and a new event is drawn from that pool.  When
a pool reaches $K$ cells it immediately splits: the child pool (1 cell) enters
the heap with its own first-event draw, and the parent pool (K-1 cells)
continues.  All pools therefore coexist and grow concurrently, unlike the
sequential mode where only one pool is active at a time.

\subsection{Why do the two modes differ so strongly?}

\paragraph{Many simultaneous small pools in parallel.}
Because all pools grow concurrently, the number of active pools at any moment
is large.  The summary statistics show that at $K=10$ the parallel run
produces $\sim 370$ pools while the sequential run also produces 200 pools but
visits them one at a time.  Crucially, in parallel mode all pools coexist on
the same time axis, so the combined ratio is an average over many small pools
\emph{at the same time}.  By the law of large numbers this average is tightly
concentrated near the mean regardless of the individual founder types.

\paragraph{Averaging over $X$- and $Y$-founders in real time.}
When $p=0$ in sequential mode, each new pool starts from a single $Y$ cell
and grows to $K$ cells in isolation before the next pool is activated.  The
result is a long stretch of near-pure $Y$ domination in the combined
trajectory.  In parallel mode, $Y$-seeded pools and residual $X$-rich parent
pools coexist simultaneously: their $X$-excesses and $Y$-excesses cancel in
the sum at every instant, leaving the combined ratio close to equilibrium.

\paragraph{Effect of $p$ is suppressed.}
Table~\ref{tab:summary_par} confirms that the parallel combined composition
is nearly independent of $p$: for $K=10$, $X/(X+Y)$ ranges from 0.569 to
0.649 across all $p$, much tighter than the sequential range of 0.210 to 0.740.

The following points compare the sequential split, parallel split, and
single-pool scenarios.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Growth shape.}  The single-pool simulation shows smooth
        near-exponential growth once the early stochastic phase passes.
        The sequential split simulation with large $K$ closely mirrors
        this shape; small $K$ produces a more stepped appearance because
        the pool queue alternates between fast-growing large pools and
        slow-starting single-cell pools.  In parallel mode all pools
        grow simultaneously, so total-count growth is always smooth and
        exponential.

  \item \textbf{Composition convergence — sequential vs.\ single pool.}
        In the single pool both types always coexist and the composition
        drifts gradually toward equilibrium.  In the sequential split
        scenario with $p = 1$, new pools are repeatedly reset to pure
        $X$, maintaining an excess of $X$ over the equilibrium value.
        With $p = 0$ the excess is in $Y$.  Only at $p = 0.5$ (or large
        $K$) does sequential behaviour closely match the single pool.

  \item \textbf{Composition convergence — parallel nearly mode-independent.}
        The parallel mode reaches near-equilibrium composition quickly
        regardless of $p$ and $K$ (Tables~\ref{tab:summary_seq}
        and~\ref{tab:summary_par}).  Concurrent growth across many pools
        averages out founder-type biases in real time.

  \item \textbf{Variance of ratios.}  Sequential splitting with small $K$
        shows substantially higher variance in the ratio trajectories
        compared with the single pool.  Each pool reset is a bottleneck
        that amplifies stochastic noise (founder effect).  Parallel mode
        suppresses this variance through averaging across pools.

  \item \textbf{Y/X transient inversion.}  With $p = 0$ in sequential
        mode, $Y/X$ can transiently exceed $1$ (more $Y$ than $X$ cells),
        something that does not occur in the single-pool scenario starting
        from pure $X$.  This effect is absent in parallel mode because
        $X$-rich parent pools counterbalance $Y$-seeded child pools at
        every instant.
\end{enumerate}

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Optimal Seeding Parameters}
\label{sec:optimal}

\subsection{Unbiased seeding condition}

In sequential mode each new pool is seeded by a single type-$X$ cell with
probability $p$ or a type-$Y$ cell with probability $1-p$.  Define:
\begin{align*}
  f_X(K) &= E\!\left[\frac{X}{X+Y}\;\bigg|\;\text{pool size}=K,\;
             \text{started from 1 }X\text{ cell}\right], \\
  f_Y(K) &= E\!\left[\frac{X}{X+Y}\;\bigg|\;\text{pool size}=K,\;
             \text{started from 1 }Y\text{ cell}\right].
\end{align*}
The expected combined $X$ fraction is
\[
  \bar{\alpha}(K,p)
  = p\,f_X(K) + (1-p)\,f_Y(K).
\]
Setting $\bar{\alpha}=\alpha_{\mathrm{eq}}$ and solving gives the
\emph{unbiased seeding probability}:
\begin{equation}
  p^*(K)
  = \frac{\alpha_{\mathrm{eq}} - f_Y(K)}{f_X(K) - f_Y(K)}.
  \label{eq:pstar}
\end{equation}
Both $f_X(K)$ and $f_Y(K)$ are estimated by Monte Carlo Gillespie
simulation of 2\,000 independent pools per value of $K$.

\subsection{Computed $p^*(K)$ and the bias surface}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{fig_pstar_K.pdf}
  \caption{Left: expected X fraction at split time, $f_X(K)$ (blue) and
           $f_Y(K)$ (red), as a function of $K$.  Both converge toward
           $\alpha_{\mathrm{eq}}\approx 0.578$ (dashed) as $K\to\infty$.
           Right: the corresponding unbiased seeding probability $p^*(K)$
           from \eqref{eq:pstar}.}
  \label{fig:pstar_K}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig_bias_surface.pdf}
  \caption{Bias $\bar{\alpha}(K,p)-\alpha_{\mathrm{eq}}$ over the full
           $(K,p)$ grid (log-$K$ axis).  Red indicates excess $X$;
           blue indicates excess $Y$.  The black curve is $p^*(K)$
           (zero-bias contour); the yellow dashed line is $p=\alpha_{\mathrm{eq}}$.}
  \label{fig:bias_surface}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:pstar_K} (right) shows a key result: $p^*(K)$ does
\emph{not} converge to $\alpha_{\mathrm{eq}}\approx 0.578$; instead it
lies persistently above 0.60 for all $K$ and is still rising toward
$\approx 0.70$–$0.75$ at $K=200$.  The reason is an \emph{asymmetry in
equilibration speed}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item An $X$-seeded pool starts with self-renewal rate $a=0.5$ and
        produces $Y$ only slowly ($b=0.1$), so at split time it has an
        $X$-excess above $\alpha_{\mathrm{eq}}$.
  \item A $Y$-seeded pool starts with self-renewal rate $d=0.4$ but can
        produce $X$ only at rate $c=0.05$ (much smaller than $b$), so its
        $X$-deficit at split time is \emph{larger in magnitude} than the
        $X$-excess of the $X$-seeded pool.
\end{itemize}

Formally, using the eigen-decomposition of $A$ (Section~\ref{sec:model}),
the deviations from equilibrium at split time decay as
$K^{-\varepsilon/\lambda_1} \approx K^{-0.32}$ in both cases, but with
unequal prefactors.  The zero-bias condition therefore converges to a
nontrivial limit $p^*(\infty) > \alpha_{\mathrm{eq}}$.

The bias surface (Figure~\ref{fig:bias_surface}) confirms that using
$p=\alpha_{\mathrm{eq}}$ (yellow dashed line) still leaves a slight
$X$-excess for all $K$, while only the $p^*(K)$ curve (black) passes
through zero bias.

\subsection{Simulation verification at $p^*(K)$}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{fig_optimal_p_sim.pdf}
  \caption{Sequential splitting: $X/(X+Y)$ trajectory for three choices
           of $p$ at $K=10$ (left) and $K=50$ (right).  The optimal
           $p^*(K)$ (dark shade) tracks the theoretical equilibrium
           most closely.}
  \label{fig:optimal_p_sim}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:optimal_p_sim} shows that seeding with $p^*(K)$ does
indeed bring the combined trajectory closest to $\alpha_{\mathrm{eq}}$,
while $p=0.5$ undershoots (too many $Y$-seeded pools) and
$p=\alpha_{\mathrm{eq}}$ is intermediate.  The improvement is especially
visible at small $K$ where the founder effect is strongest.

\subsection{Variance: the $K$-bias trade-off}

For fixed total $N$, the combined-ratio variance scales as
\[
  \mathrm{Var}\!\left(\frac{X}{X+Y}\right)
  \;\approx\;
  \frac{K}{N}\;\mathrm{Var}\!\left(\frac{X}{X+Y}\;\bigg|\;\text{size}=K\right)
  \;\propto\;
  \frac{K^{1-2\varepsilon/\lambda_1}}{N}
  \;\propto\;
  \frac{K^{0.354}}{N}.
\]
Because the exponent $1-2\varepsilon/\lambda_1 \approx 0.354>0$, the
variance is \emph{monotonically increasing in $K$} for our parameter
values.  There is therefore no interior variance-minimising $K$: small
$K$ always reduces variance (more pools $\Rightarrow$ more averaging),
at the cost of stronger founder-effect bias.

\paragraph{Practical recommendation.}
Use $p = p^*(K)$ from \eqref{eq:pstar} to eliminate bias regardless of
$K$.  Given the monotone variance result, prefer small $K$ if variance is
the priority, and use $p^*(K)\approx 0.60$–$0.66$.  If equilibration
fidelity within each pool is the priority (e.g.\ to avoid transient
inversion of $Y/X$), prefer larger $K$ and note that the bias correction
is less critical since all pools nearly equilibrate before splitting.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Founder effect and bottlenecks}

The dominant feature of the split simulation — cell-type domination within
each pool — is a manifestation of the \emph{founder effect}: when a new
population is established from a single individual, the genetic (here: type)
composition of the founder disproportionately shapes the descendant
population.  The strength of the effect is controlled by $K$:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Small $K$: strong bottlenecks, strong founder effect, high between-pool
        heterogeneity in composition.
  \item Large $K$: weak bottlenecks, composition equilibrates within each pool
        before the next split, between-pool heterogeneity low.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Invariance of growth rate}

Because splits only redistribute existing cells (no net creation or
destruction), the dominant eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ and therefore the
exponential growth rate of the total population are the same in all
scenarios.  What changes is how quickly the composition vector $(X, Y)/N$
approaches the Perron eigenvector $\approx (0.578, 0.422)$.

\subsection{Practical interpretation}

If this model represents, for example, stem-cell passage in a culture dish,
the splitting threshold $K$ is the passage size and $p$ is the fraction of
stem cells (type $X$) deliberately maintained during passaging.  The analysis
shows that:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Passaging at a large $K$ is more ``self-correcting'' — the culture
        naturally drifts toward equilibrium composition.
  \item Biasing $p$ toward 1 (seeding with $X$) enriches $X$ in subsequent
        passages but does not prevent $Y$ from accumulating in the long run.
  \item Biasing $p$ toward 0 can temporarily invert the composition
        ($Y > X$) but the system eventually returns toward equilibrium as
        $Y$-seeded pools generate $X$ cells through $Y \to X$ events.
\end{itemize}
